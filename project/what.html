What is Universal Design?

Universal Design is creating a product that can be accessed, utilized, and understood by any person, regardless of their age, ability, or disability. It is created on the view that the spectrum of peopleâ€™s abilities is wide and our abilities can change over time. We can use universal design to meet the needs of all users to the greatest extent without having to adapt or make designs for different populations. This not only makes it easier for the user themselves, but us as web developers and designers as it sets out guidelines for how to design a universally accessible website so different versions do not have to be adapted.

In universal design, we consider the diverse needs and abilities of all users in creation to meet all users needs. If our products are universally designed, and are accessible, usable it is a good design.

7 Principles of Universal Design

In 1997, Ronald Mace and his colleagues at North Carolina State University developed the 7 Principles of Universal Design

  1. Equitable Use

    Design is useful and marketable to people with diverse abilities.

    • Provide same means of use for all users, identical when possible, equivalent when not
    • Avoid segregating or stigmatizing any users
    • Provisions for privacy, security, and safety should be equally available to all users
    • Make the design appealing to all users.

  2. Flexibility in Use

    The design accommodates a wide range of individual preferences and abilities

    • Provide choice in methods of use
    • Accommodate right- or left-handed access and use
    • Facilitate the user's accuracy and precision
    • Provide adaptability to the user's pace

  3. Simple and Intuitive Use

    Use of the design is easy to understand, regardless of the user's experience, knowledge, language skills, or current concentration level

    • Eliminate unnecessary complexity
    • Be consistent with user expectations and intuition
    • Accommodate a wide range of literacy and language skills
    • Arrange information consistent with its importance
    • Provide effective prompting and feedback during and after task completion

  4. Perceptible Information

    The design communicates necessary information effectively to the user, regardless of ambient conditions or the user's sensory abilities

    • Use different modes (pictorial, verbal, tactile) for redundant presentation of essential information
    • Provide adequate contrast between essential information and its surroundings
    • Maximize "legibility" of essential information
    • Differentiate elements in ways that can be described (i.e., make it easy to give instructions or directions)
    • Provide compatibility with a variety of techniques or devices used by people with sensory limitations

  5. Tolerance for Error

    The design minimizes hazards and the adverse consequences of accidental or unintended actions

    • Arrange elements to minimize hazards and errors: most used elements, most accessible; hazardous elements eliminated, isolated, or shielded
    • Provide warnings of hazards and errors
    • Provide fail safe features
    • Discourage unconscious action in tasks that require vigilance

  6. Low Physical Effort

    The design can be used efficiently and comfortably and with a minimum of fatigue

    • Allow user to maintain a neutral body position
    • Use reasonable operating forces
    • Minimize repetitive actions
    • Minimize sustained physical effort

  7. Size and Space for Approach and Use

    Appropriate size and space is provided for approach, reach, manipulation, and use regardless of user's body size, posture, or mobility

    • Provide a clear line of sight to important elements for any seated or standing user
    • Make reach to all components comfortable for any seated or standing user
    • Accommodate variations in hand and grip size
    • Provide adequate space for the use of assistive devices or personal assistance

Norman's Design Concepts

  • Visibility

    If functions are visible, users are more likely know what to do. If they are hidden, users can struggle to find them and use them

  • Feedback

    When a user completes an action, feedback can send back information on what the user did, so they are aware if their action is successful or not

  • Constraints

    A user is restricted from doing certain actions to prevent errors, misuse, or damage to the device or user

  • Mapping

    The presentation of controls that will mirror the operation that is going to be performed. It can also with position/proximity indicate the device/component associated with the control

  • Consistency

    Design interfaces to have similar operations and similar elements to achieve similar tasks

WCAG

Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are guidelines that define how to make web content accessible to people with disabilities, other individuals with abilities that change due to aging, and can help improve usability for users in general. WCAG helps to develop web accessibility standards for web and software developers.

WCAG Principles

  • Perceivable

    Information and user interface components are presented in a way users can see/hear

  • Operable

    Designed so users can interact with them and support assistive technologies

  • Understandable

    Information and operation of user interface clearly and consistently communicates content so it is readable

  • Robust

    Content must be written so it can be interpreted reliably by wide variety of user agents, including assistive technologies
